Getting into college

Build a standout extracurricular profile.

Your activities tell colleges who you are outside the classroom. They show leadership, commitment, and character — things grades and test scores can't fully capture. Here's how to build a profile that actually stands out.

The truth about ECs

What colleges are actually looking for

In a 2023 survey by the National Association for College Admission Counseling, over 51% of colleges rated extracurricular activities as moderately or considerably important to their admissions decisions. But the way most students approach ECs is completely backwards.

51%

of colleges surveyed by NACAC rate extracurricular activities as "moderately" or "considerably" important to admissions decisions. At selective schools, that number is even higher — and the weight shifts dramatically toward leadership, impact, and commitment over participation alone.

Depth beats breadth
Admissions officers are not counting how many clubs you joined. They are looking for sustained commitment and genuine growth. Three or four activities you have dedicated real time to over multiple years are worth far more than ten clubs you listed for the sake of filling a page. Depth of involvement signals that you will bring that same commitment to college.
Leadership and impact matter more than the activity itself
Playing varsity soccer is fine. Being elected captain and leading your team to a regional championship is a different kind of entry. Founding a club is more impressive than joining one. Starting something, leading something, or measurably improving something shows initiative — one of the qualities colleges most want to see.
Authenticity over prestige
Colleges can tell when a student joined Model UN because they thought it would look good versus because they are genuinely passionate about international policy. Choose activities that align with your actual interests. Your enthusiasm shows through in your essays, interviews, and the way you describe your experiences — and it makes your entire application more cohesive.
Your activities should tell a story
The strongest applications have a through-line. Your ECs, your essay, your intended major, and your goals should feel like they come from the same person with a coherent set of interests and values. An aspiring engineer who does robotics, tutors math, and volunteers at a science camp tells a cleaner story than a student with activities scattered across a dozen unrelated categories.
Jobs and family responsibilities count
If you worked part-time to help support your family, cared for a sibling, or ran a small business, those belong on your activity list. Admissions officers understand that not every student has access to the same opportunities — and demonstrating responsibility, work ethic, and real-world experience is often more impressive than another club membership.

How to list your activities

Writing your Common App activity list

The Common App gives you space for up to 10 activities, each with a 150-character description. 150 characters is tight. Every word has to earn its place. Here is how to make the most of it.

How to write a strong 150-character activity description
1
Lead with what you did, not the club's name or mission
Bad: "Member of the National Honor Society, which recognizes academic achievement." Good: "Selected for academic excellence; tutored 12 underclassmen weekly in math and science."
2
Include numbers and specifics whenever possible
Quantify your impact: how many people you led, how much money you raised, how many hours per week you committed, what place you finished. "Raised $4,200 for local food bank" beats "fundraising for charity" every time.
3
List in order of importance to you, not chronologically
Put your most meaningful or impressive activity first. Admissions officers read quickly — the top of your list gets the most attention. Your number-one slot should reflect what matters most to you or where you had the greatest impact.
4
Include hours per week and weeks per year accurately
Common App asks for both. Be honest but thorough — include travel time for sports, practice time, and preparation time, not just the activity itself. Underreporting makes strong commitments look casual.
5
Use the Additional Information section for overflow
If you have more than 10 activities or need more than 150 characters to describe a particularly important one, use the 650-word Additional Information section. This is also where you can explain unusual circumstances — like why you only participated in something for one year.
What to avoid

Common extracurricular mistakes

Joining clubs just to add them to the list

Admissions officers read thousands of applications. They can tell when a student joined something in senior year solely to check a box. A club you attended three meetings of is not a meaningful extracurricular.

Only list activities you actually participated in meaningfully and consistently. Three genuine activities beat ten superficial ones.

Listing memberships without noting what you actually did

Being a "member" of a club says almost nothing. What did you contribute? What role did you play? Did you organize anything, present anything, or lead anyone?

Always describe what you did within the activity, not just that you participated. Use action verbs: led, founded, organized, coached, designed, raised.

Quitting activities to "focus on grades"

Colleges want to see sustained commitment. Dropping activities because they became inconvenient signals that you give up when things get hard — exactly the opposite of what admissions officers want to see.

Choose fewer activities if needed, but commit to them for multiple years. Longevity is a signal of character.

Starting everything junior year

A spike of activity in junior year right before applications looks calculated. Colleges look at the full four years of high school, and a sudden burst of involvement in year three stands out for the wrong reasons.

Start freshman or sophomore year. Even two or three years of commitment in a single activity is far stronger than six months of frantic involvement in many.
