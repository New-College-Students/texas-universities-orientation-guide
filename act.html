Your 2026 Enhanced ACT prep guide.

The ACT changed significantly in 2025 and 2026. This guide covers the new format, section-by-section strategies, a study plan, and every verified free resource available right now.

Important: The ACT has a new format for 2026. The Enhanced ACT is now shorter (about 2 hours for the core), Science is now optional and no longer counted in your composite score, and the composite is calculated from English, Math, and Reading only. Do not use pre-2025 practice tests for timing drills — the question counts are different. Sources: Kaplan, ACT.org

Know the format

The 2026 Enhanced ACT — what changed and what didn't

The ACT completed its rollout of the Enhanced format in Spring 2026 for all school-day testing. The core test is now three sections. Science and Writing are optional add-ons you choose when registering.

Section Questions Time Notes
English 50 questions 35 minutes Grammar, punctuation, sentence structure, and rhetorical skills
Math 45 questions 50 minutes 4 answer choices (down from 5); Desmos calculator available on digital version
Reading 36 questions 40 minutes 4 passages; measures comprehension, inference, and analysis
Science Optional +$4 40 questions 40 minutes No longer in composite score. Generates a separate Science score and STEM score
Writing Optional +$25 1 essay prompt 40 minutes Generates a separate Writing score and ELA score. Check if your target schools require it
ACT vs SAT

Which test is right for you?

Both tests are now about 2 hours long and accepted equally by virtually every US college. The real difference is in style and structure. Take a full diagnostic of each before committing to one.

Choose the ACT if...

  • You prefer a paper-and-pencil test experience (available at select centers)
  • You are strong in geometry and trigonometry, which the ACT tests more heavily than the SAT
  • You get anxious about adaptive testing where difficulty shifts between modules
  • You want to showcase a strong Science score separately for STEM programs
  • You tend to read faster and can work through longer passages efficiently

Choose the SAT if...

  • You are strongest in algebra and comfortable using Desmos to solve problems graphically
  • You prefer shorter, more targeted reading passages over longer narrative texts
  • You are comfortable with an adaptive format where Module 2 difficulty adjusts to your performance
  • Your target schools superscore the SAT (many more do than the ACT)
  • You want built-in Desmos on every math question without needing a paper test workaround
Section strategies

What to focus on in each section

The Enhanced ACT gives you more time per question than the old format. The strategy shift is from speed to accuracy — slow down, eliminate, and commit rather than rushing through.

01

English — edit, don't rewrite

Most English questions ask you to improve a passage, not rewrite it. "No Change" is correct more often than students expect. Always read the full sentence in context before choosing an answer, not just the underlined portion.

02

Math — use Desmos on digital

If you are taking the digital ACT, a built-in Desmos calculator is available for the entire Math section. Use it to graph equations, find intersections, and check your algebra. Many problems that take 90 seconds by hand take 15 seconds in Desmos.

03

Reading — read the questions first

Skim the questions for a passage before reading it so you know what details to look for. For paired-passage questions, read both passages before answering any questions about their relationship — do not answer as you go.

04

Science (optional) — it's reading, not science

The Science section tests your ability to read graphs, tables, and data — not memorized science facts. Practice interpreting charts quickly. Most answers are directly in the figures, not in scientific knowledge you have to recall.

05

Pace yourself — more time per question

The Enhanced ACT gives you roughly 22% more time per question than the old format. Use it. Rushing is now a bigger mistake than it used to be — careless errors from speed cost more points than running out of time.

06

Never leave a blank — no penalty

There is no wrong-answer penalty on the ACT. If time is running out, fill in every remaining bubble with the same letter. Statistically, random guessing on 10 questions will get you roughly 2.5 correct, which is better than zero.

Study plan

A plan for every timeline

Studying 10 hours per week for 3 months can produce around a 4-point composite gain. Studying 20 hours per week can boost scores by 9 or more points. Always start with an official diagnostic — not a third-party one.

Month 1
Download the official Preparing for the ACT 2025-2026 PDF from act.org and take it as a timed diagnostic. Do not study before — you need an honest baseline. Grade every section and identify your two weakest skill areas.
Months 2–3
Study 45–60 minutes daily using ACT Academy (free at act.org) and targeted drills on your weak areas. Keep an error log: every missed question gets a written note explaining why you missed it.
Month 4+
Take a full-length practice test every 3–4 weeks. Compare scores section by section, not just composite. Shift from single-topic drills to mixed timed sets that mirror real test pacing.
Final 2 weeks
One final full-length test about 10 days before your exam. Then taper: light review of your error log only, no new content. Get your timing and test-day logistics confirmed well before the night before.
Week 1
Take the official Preparing for the ACT PDF as a timed diagnostic. Identify your weakest section and your top three weak skill types within it.
Weeks 2–4
Study 60 minutes daily. Use ACT Academy for targeted skill drills. Do not drill randomly — spend 70% of your time on your identified weak areas. Keep an error log every session.
Weeks 5–6
Take the official 2026 ACT Practice Test 2 (available at act.org) as a second full-length timed test. Compare section scores to your baseline. Adjust your final week's focus based on where you have improved and where you haven't.
Final days
Review your error log. No new content. Confirm your admission ticket, ID, testing device charge, and directions to the test center.
Week 1
Take the official Preparing for the ACT PDF diagnostic on Day 1. Spend the rest of the week drilling your single weakest section for 60 minutes daily using ACT Academy.
Week 2
Continue targeted drills. Focus on your two or three highest-frequency weak question types — not the whole section. Do 20 timed practice questions per session, then review every miss carefully.
Week 3
Take a second full-length timed practice test. Use the results to identify any remaining gaps. Spend the rest of the week on those specific gaps only.
Week 4
Light review only — no marathon sessions. Review your error log once. Confirm all test-day logistics. Stop studying 2 days before the exam and prioritize sleep.
5 days out
Take one final full-length timed practice test using an official source. Review every mistake the same day. No new content introduction after this point.
3–4 days out
Skim your error log for patterns. Light targeted review of your weakest skill only — 30 minutes maximum. Everything new you try to learn now will cause more confusion than clarity.
2 days out
Confirm your printed admission ticket, acceptable photo ID, No. 2 pencils, approved calculator, snacks, and directions to your test center. Charge your device if taking the digital test.
Night before
No studying. Lay everything out. Get a full night of sleep — this matters more for your score than any last-minute cramming.
