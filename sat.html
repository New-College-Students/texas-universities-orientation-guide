Know the format

It's digital, adaptive, and shorter than you think

The SAT is fully digital and taken through an app called Bluebook. It has two sections, Reading & Writing and Math, each split into two timed modules. Your performance on the first module of each section determines whether you see an easier or harder second module, and the harder module is where the highest scores come from.

01

It's adaptive

Each section has two modules. Do well on Module 1 and you get a harder, higher-scoring Module 2. Don't sandbag the first module thinking you'll "save energy."

02

Desmos is built in

A full graphing calculator is available for every Math question. Learn to use it well: many problems that take a minute by hand take seconds in Desmos.

03

No penalty for guessing

There's no penalty for wrong answers, so never leave a question blank. An educated guess costs you nothing and might gain you points.

04

Practice on Bluebook

Always practice using the actual Bluebook app, not paper printouts. The interface, the annotation tool, and the pacing all feel different until you've used the real thing.

Math strategy

High-frequency topics & quick wins

Digital SAT Math questions fall into four content areas. Mastering the highest-frequency topics and knowing when to reach for Desmos versus solving by hand is often the difference between a good score and a great one.

Linear equations & systems
Highest-frequency topic on the test. Practice solving by substitution, elimination, and graphing in Desmos until it's automatic.
Functions & nonlinear equations
Quadratics, exponentials, and function notation appear constantly. Use Desmos to instantly see roots, vertices, and intercepts.
Ratios, rates & percentages
Common in word problems. Set up your equation carefully before solving, since most errors come from misreading the question, not the math.
Data analysis & statistics
Mean, median, scatterplots, and probability. Read every chart and table carefully; the trap answers usually come from a misread axis or label.
Reading & Writing strategy

Pace yourself and trust the patterns

Reading & Writing questions are short, single-passage questions rather than long reading sets. Speed and pattern recognition matter as much as comprehension.

05

Aim for 75 seconds per question

If you're stuck past 75 seconds, flag it and move on. You can return to flagged questions before the module ends.

06

Reread the sentence, not just the blank

For word-in-context questions, substitute each answer choice back into the full sentence rather than guessing from the word alone.

07

Use the annotation tool

Bluebook lets you highlight and underline text. Practice using it during prep so it's second nature by test day, not something you're learning live.

08

Keep a weekly error log

Every time you miss a question, write down why. Patterns show up fast, and fixing the same mistake type beats grinding random questions.

Study plan

Pick a timeline that matches your test date

Most students need 20–40 hours of focused prep for a ~100-point gain, and 40–80 hours for a ~200-point gain. Pick the plan that fits how much time you have left.

Month 1
Take a full-length Bluebook diagnostic test to establish your baseline score. Don't study beforehand, you want an honest starting point.
Months 2–3
Identify your two weakest specific question types (not just "Math" or "Reading"). Study 45–60 minutes daily using Khan Academy's Official SAT Practice.
Months 4–5
Take a full-length practice test every 4–6 weeks to track improvement. Build a weekly error log and review every mistake, not just the score.
Month 6
Shift to timed, mixed practice sets that mirror real test pacing. Take your final full-length practice test 1–2 weeks before test day.
Week 1
Take a Bluebook diagnostic test. Identify your two weakest specific skills, not broad categories.
Weeks 2–6
Study 45–60 minutes daily on your weak areas using Khan Academy and the Question Bank. Keep an error log every session.
Weeks 7–10
Take a full timed practice test every 2 weeks. Shift toward timed mixed sets rather than single-topic drills.
Weeks 11–12
Final review of your error log. One last full-length practice test about a week before your actual exam, then taper down.
Week 1
Diagnostic test on Bluebook. Spend the rest of the week drilling your single weakest area using the Question Bank, filtered by skill.
Week 2
Daily 60-minute sessions: 20 questions, then 40 minutes reviewing every miss and writing why you missed it.
Week 3
Take one full-length timed practice test. Focus remaining study time on your two or three highest-frequency weak spots.
Week 4
Light review only. Walk through Desmos shortcuts and pacing strategy. Don't cram new content the final 2–3 days.
5 days out
One last full-length practice test under real timed conditions. Review every mistake the same day.
3–4 days out
Light, targeted review of your weakest skill only. No new content, no marathon study sessions.
2 days out
Confirm your testing device works with Bluebook, charge it fully, and review your admission ticket and ID requirements.
Night before
No studying. Get a full night of sleep. Lay out everything you need: ID, admission ticket, charged device, snacks, water.

Where to actually practice

Official College Board resources should be the foundation of your prep since they're the closest thing to the real test. Free third-party resources are excellent supplements once you've exhausted the official material.
