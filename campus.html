Step 1

How to pick professors

The professor matters more than the time slot. A great professor can make a hard class manageable. A poor one can make an easy class miserable. Here is how to research before you register.

Start with RateMyProfessors

Go to ratemyprofessors.com and search your school. Read the reviews critically — look for patterns across multiple students, not just one or two outliers. Pay attention to comments about clarity, grading fairness, and how available they are outside class.

Check your school's own evaluations

Most universities publish student course evaluations through their own portal (UT Austin calls it CIS, other schools have similar systems). These are more structured and reliable than third-party sites. Search your school's name plus "course instructor survey" or "student evaluations."

Ask current students directly

Reddit communities for your school (search r/UTDallas, r/UTAustin, etc.) are full of honest, recent professor reviews. Ask specifically about the class you are taking — a professor great for 200-level may be brutal at 300-level.

Attend the first class before committing

Most schools allow you to attend a class during add/drop without being enrolled. Go to the first session of any class you are unsure about before the deadline. You will know within 20 minutes whether the professor's style works for you.

Read the syllabus before registering

Many professors post their syllabi online before the semester starts. Search your school's course schedule for a syllabus link, or email the department. Knowing the grading breakdown and workload before you register is a significant advantage.

Grade distribution matters

Some schools publish historical grade distributions by professor and course. Search your school's name plus "grade distribution" — UT Austin, Texas A&M, and several others make this public. A class with 80% A's and B's and one with 30% is a very different experience.

Step 2

Navigate add/drop

Add/drop is the window at the start of each semester when you can change your schedule without academic penalty. Knowing exactly when it opens and closes — and how to use it strategically — is one of the most valuable things you can know as a freshman.

Find your school's exact add/drop deadline — it varies
Most schools have a short add/drop window of 5–12 business days after the first day of class. After that, dropping a course may result in a "W" (withdrawal) on your transcript, a partial refund, or no refund at all. This date lives in your school's academic calendar — know it before the semester starts.
Use the first week to shop classes, not just attend them
The first week of class is your free trial period. Attend the first session of any class you are on the fence about — even if you are not yet enrolled or are on a waitlist. Most professors allow this, and many waitlisted students get a spot by showing up in person and talking to the professor directly.
Add before you drop — don't accidentally go below full-time
If you need to swap courses, add the new one first before dropping the old one. Dropping below full-time status (typically 12 credit hours) can affect your financial aid, health insurance coverage under a parent's plan, and housing eligibility. Check your specific aid requirements before making any schedule change.
Email the professor to get off a waitlist
If a course you need is full, email the professor directly — not just the department. Introduce yourself, explain why you need the course, and ask if they anticipate any spots opening. Attend the first class in person regardless. Waitlists often clear during add/drop as students swap schedules, and professors have discretion to add students directly in many cases.
A late withdrawal looks different than never enrolling
A "W" on your transcript is not the end of the world, but too many raise flags for graduate schools and competitive internship programs. One W for a genuine reason is fine. Four W's across two years tells a different story. Withdraw if you need to — but be intentional about it.
Step 3

Build and protect your GPA

Your freshman year GPA sets the foundation for everything after it — transfer applications, graduate school, scholarships, and internships. Here is how to approach it strategically from day one.

Know your major's GPA floor

Most competitive programs have a minimum GPA to stay in the major — business, nursing, and engineering schools are especially strict. Know yours before the semester starts, not when you get your first grade back. Talk to your advisor in the first two weeks.

Front-load your easy courses

Freshman year is the hardest transition academically. If you have flexibility in your schedule, lean toward courses you are genuinely stronger in during your first semester. A strong GPA early gives you cushion for harder upper-division courses later.

Go to office hours before you need to

Most students only go to office hours when they are already struggling. Go during the first two weeks when you do not need anything specific. Introduce yourself. When grades become important, professors remember the students who showed up early — and that relationship matters.

Treat studying like a job

College professors typically expect 2–3 hours of work outside class for every hour in class. A 15-credit semester means roughly 30–45 hours per week of study time on top of class time. Build that into your schedule from week one instead of catching up on week eight.

Use the tutoring center — it is free

Every college has a free tutoring center that most students never visit. For STEM courses especially, regular tutoring sessions can be the difference between a B and an A. Go before an exam, not the night before. Walk in, find out what subjects are covered, and schedule a session in the first month.

Track your GPA in real time

Use our GPA calculator to understand exactly where you stand at any point in the semester. Knowing that a B on the next exam drops you from a 3.7 to a 3.4 is much more motivating than just knowing you "need to do better."

Step 4

Find internships — earlier than you think

Most freshmen assume internships are for juniors. They are not. Freshman and sophomore internships exist, and students who start early graduate with a full work history. Here is how to approach the timeline.

September
Set up LinkedIn and clean up your resume
You do not need work experience to have a LinkedIn profile. Add your school, your major, any clubs you have joined, and a professional photo. Your school's career center will review your resume for free — go during the first month, not right before an application deadline.
October
Attend every career fair — even as a freshman
Most campus career fairs happen in September and October. Go even if you are not ready to apply anywhere. Introduce yourself, take business cards, and practice your pitch. Recruiters remember faces — showing up as a freshman and returning as a junior is a powerful narrative.
November
Apply for summer internships — they open earlier than you expect
Large company summer internship applications open in September and October for the following summer. Many freshmen miss this entirely. Start watching LinkedIn, Handshake (your school's internship portal), and company career pages in October. Competitive programs fill months before the summer.
January
Cold email professors for research opportunities
Research assistant positions are one of the most overlooked early-career opportunities. Email 4–6 professors in your department whose work interests you. Keep it brief: who you are, what you find interesting about their research, and that you would like to help. Three or four will ignore you. One or two will say yes — and a research role changes your resume completely.
Spring
Apply for local and small company internships
Large companies often require sophomore status or above. Local businesses, startups, and nonprofits hire freshmen regularly and often provide more meaningful hands-on work. Use your school's Handshake portal, LinkedIn's "Easy Apply" filter, and your own network. Your first internship does not have to be at a Fortune 500 company — it just has to be something.
Step 5

Campus resources most students never use

You are already paying for these through tuition and fees. The students who use them consistently have a measurable advantage over those who do not.

Career center
Free resume reviews, mock interviews, internship and job postings through Handshake, and recruiter connections. Most students visit once before graduation. The students who visit every semester have consistently stronger outcomes. Walk in during the first month of freshman year.
Writing center
Free one-on-one feedback on any paper before you submit it. Not just for struggling writers — strong writers use writing centers too, because an outside eye always catches something. Book appointments at least 48 hours in advance; they fill up before major deadlines.
Tutoring and academic support centers
Free tutoring for math, science, writing, and often major-specific subjects. Drop-in and scheduled appointments both available at most schools. Go before you are struggling, not after your first bad grade.
Counseling center
Free or low-cost mental health support. Waitlists can be several weeks long at large schools — register early in the semester even if you do not need immediate support, so you are already in the system if you do later. Freshman year is one of the most common times students experience anxiety and burnout.
Library research services
Librarians are research specialists and will help you find sources, navigate academic databases, and structure a research paper for free. Most students use the library only to print things or find a quiet seat. Research librarians are one of the most underused resources on campus.
Free software through your school
Most universities provide free access to Microsoft 365, Adobe Creative Cloud, MATLAB, SPSS, and other professional tools through your student email. Check your school's IT or software licensing page — students spend hundreds of dollars on software they could have gotten free.
Step 6

Common freshman mistakes — and how to avoid them

These are not edge cases. Every single one of these happens to a large percentage of freshmen every year.

Skipping class "just once"

College professors often do not take attendance. That makes it easy to justify skipping — until you have missed three weeks without realizing it. Missing one lecture in a fast-moving course can take 4–5 hours to self-teach.

Treat every class like a meeting with your boss. You can miss some, but not without a plan for catching up the same day.

Waiting until finals to study

College exams cover more material at a deeper level than high school tests. Cramming the night before works poorly even in high school — in college it often fails completely.

Review notes the same day as each class. It takes 15 minutes per class and dramatically improves retention before any studying even starts.

Not reading the syllabus

The syllabus contains every deadline, grading breakdown, late policy, and exam date. Most students glance at it on day one and never open it again — then miss a deadline they "didn't know about."

On the first day of every class, transfer every deadline from every syllabus into your phone calendar. All of them, in one sitting.

Taking 18 credits the first semester

Overloading in freshman year is one of the most common GPA killers. The jump in difficulty from high school to college is real — adding extra courses on top makes it worse.

Take 15 credits your first semester unless you have a specific reason not to. You can always add more once you know how college-level workloads actually feel.

Never going to office hours

Office hours exist for one reason: so professors can help you. Most students never show up. The ones who do get better explanations, earlier warning about exam topics, and a professor who actually knows their name.

Go to every professor's office hours at least once in the first month. Even if you have no specific question — say hello and introduce yourself.

Ignoring your email for weeks

Financial aid holds, registration blocks, scholarship deadlines, and professor announcements all go to your student email. Students who do not check it miss things that cost them money and opportunities.

Set up your student email on your phone and check it every morning. It takes 90 seconds and prevents a significant number of avoidable problems.
